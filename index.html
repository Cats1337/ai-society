AI, Age, and the Illusion of Safety

The UK Online Safety Act gives Ofcom power to demand ‚Äúproportionate‚Äù age checks. Platforms lean on AI tools like face estimation, document scans, and behavior modeling to avoid full ID uploads. In the process they build an infrastructure for routine identity checks online.

Quick answers

What is happening, in five bullets

Use these as a fast primer or slide starter.

The Act

What is the UK Online Safety Act?

Law that obliges services to reduce exposure to illegal and high risk content. Ofcom can fine and block access. Age assurance sits at the center for ‚Äúprimary priority‚Äù content.

Platform duties

What does it require of platforms?

Platforms must prevent minors from reaching restricted content using proportionate measures, document their risk assessments, and show workable enforcement to Ofcom.

Implementation

How are platforms using AI?

Face based age estimation, ID plus selfie flows with liveness checks, behavioral pattern models, and device or network signals combined into risk scores.

Failures

Where are systems failing?

Bias for darker skin and trans users, higher error near age thresholds, privacy risks from biometric capture, and spoofing workarounds with false positives and false negatives.

Implications

Broader societal impacts?

Normalizes biometric checkpoints, erodes anonymity, outsources regulation to platforms, and adds extra friction for marginalized and undocumented people.

Policy backdrop

What the Online Safety Act expects

The Act is framed as a duty of care. Ofcom can require platforms to stop minors from reaching ‚Äúprimary priority content‚Äù. Age checks must be proportionate, but vendors are selling AI shortcuts that come with their own risks.

The requirement

Age assurance on demand

Sites must show that they take appropriate measures to keep minors out of restricted areas. Enforcement can escalate to fines and access blocking if they cannot demonstrate this.

Regulator

Ofcom's leverage

The provisional notice to 4chan shows that Ofcom intends to test its powers, using specific cases to signal how it will treat illegal and harmful content duties.

Scope creep

Beyond pornography

Pornography was the early focus, but music videos, livestreams, and ‚Äúborderline‚Äù social spaces sit in scope as risk categories expand. Age checks move from adult sites into everyday browsing.

Mechanisms

How AI based age detection works

Systems blend multiple signals. Each signal trades convenience against accuracy, privacy, and fairness. Together they create a gate that can feel simple for users but complex behind the scenes.

Face estimation

Facial analysis

Neural networks predict age from selfies or live video. Performance drops for darker skin tones, trans users, and teens near 18. Lighting and camera quality matter in ways that rarely show up in glossy demos.

ID + liveness

Document verification

OCR reads passports or IDs, face match compares an ID photo to a live selfie, and liveness checks try to resist replays. Sensitive data flows through even when vendors say ‚Äúverification only‚Äù.

Behavioral

Pattern analysis

Models infer age from typing cadence, scroll speed, payment history, or playtime. This logic is opaque and risks false blocks for neurodivergent users or anyone with unusual usage patterns.

Device signals

Network and device

SIM checks, carrier age flags, device fingerprints, and account histories. Cross platform tracking risk grows as these signals are combined into unified profiles.

Risk score

Hybrid decisions

Vendors output confidence scores that prompt platforms to allow, ask for more proof, or block. Thresholds encode policy choices and shift who bears friction and error.

Document pipeline

Step by step flow (for diagrams)

  1. Upload ID such as a passport or driver license.
  2. OCR extracts the birth date and checks document structure and validity.
  3. Face match compares the ID photo to a live selfie from the user.
  4. Liveness checks look for signs of printed photos or replayed video.
  5. Decision returned: access granted, denied, or escalated for review.

Paper 2 describes this end to end identity pipeline. Paper 5 covers age estimation models, datasets, and evaluation metrics.

+ Technical sketch of facial age estimation
  1. Face detection and alignment crop and normalize faces for pose and scale.
  2. Convolutional or transformer based networks extract feature embeddings from the aligned face.
  3. A regression or classification head maps those embeddings to an age value or age bracket.
  4. Systems apply thresholds such as ‚Äúaccept if over 23 with 95 percent confidence‚Äù.
  5. Bias creeps in through training data and architecture choices, which rarely match the full population being judged.
Performance and harm

Where the systems succeed, and where they fail

Succeeding (narrowly)
  • Simple over or under checks can work when faces resemble training data and conditions are ideal.
  • Document plus liveness flows reduce casual spoofing compared to ‚ÄúI am 18‚Äù click through boxes.
  • Hybrid signals can flag reused IDs or obvious bot driven attempts in mass traffic.
Failing (more often)
  • Bias: higher error for Black and Brown faces, trans users, elders, and teens near 18, as shown in work on human and AI age perception (Paper 4).
  • Privacy: biometric data is collected; vendors may retain images for model improvement even when they market a pure ‚Äúverification only‚Äù service.
  • Workarounds: VPNs, borrowed devices, and spoofed selfies still slip through, while false positives lock out legitimate adults.
  • Opaque accountability: proprietary models resist independent audits and errors are hard to contest.
Misclassification
Adult flagged as a child
An 18 year old with a younger appearance is repeatedly blocked from platforms. They are pushed into ID verification that may require parental documents or locked out if they lack access to official ID.
Bias
Over reach
Mandatory ID in unsafe homes
Young people in abusive or queerphobic households can only access support forums by using a parent ID. Safety tools end up removing their last private channels instead of protecting them.
Harm
Data spill
Leaked biometric archive
Years of face scans and ID images are exposed in a breach. Attackers gain both birth dates and reusable face prints that can be repurposed in other systems.

Systemic risk: A one percent lab error rate turns into tens of thousands of wrong decisions at platform scale, and those mistakes fall hardest on people who already face discrimination and over policing.
Societal layer

Implications beyond the login screen

Age checks do more than gate adult content. They reshape norms around identity, speech, and who gets to participate online without being logged and scored.

Responsibilization

Outsourced enforcement

Governments push platforms to police users. Vendors sell compliance scores that move risk downstream. Jarvie and Renaud describe this shift as responsibilization, where technical checks stand in for broader social commitments to children.

Surveillance creep

Normalization of biometrics

Once face scans and ID checks feel routine at sign in, it becomes easier to extend them into workplaces, schools, travel, and public spaces, especially if data is retained and cross matched.

Anonymity loss

Identity demanded by default

Mandatory verification erodes pseudonymity used by activists, queer youth, sex workers, and whistleblowers. Speech becomes tied to risk scores and logs that can be accessed later.

Marginalized impacts

Uneven friction

Undocumented people, migrants, unhoused users, and those with non conforming gender presentation face higher denial rates, more intrusive checks, and greater exposure when they try to participate online.

‚ùñ
Children's rights framing: UNICEF and child rights groups stress that children have rights to participation, privacy, and information, not just a right to protection. Systems that see them only as potential victims risk erasing their agency and voice.
Pathways

Alternatives and safeguards

Proportionate design
  • Age range credentials that only reveal ‚Äúover 18‚Äù without storing full ID data on every platform.
  • On device inference where possible, with biometric captures deleted immediately after a decision.
  • Independent audits and public reporting of false accept and false reject rates.
Human centered
  • Digital literacy and voluntary education instead of making surveillance the default safety tool.
  • Appeal paths for false positives, including human review for edge cases and vulnerable users.
  • Spaces that explicitly avoid compelled ID, especially for support communities and sensitive speech.
Policy realism
  • Recognize limits: AI age checks can reduce exposure but will never fully stop determined access.
  • Prevent scope drift by confining mandatory checks to clearly high risk categories.
  • Require clear deletion windows, bans on secondary use, and strict limits on law enforcement access.
